The Universe

The universe encompasses all matter, energy, space, and time—nearly 14 billion years old, and still expanding. From stars and galaxies to dark matter and cosmic voids, it challenges our understanding and drives exploration on scales both vast and infinitesimal.

Origin: The Big Bang & Inflation

The universe began 13.8 billion years ago with the Big Bang—a rapid expansion from an ultra-dense, hot state. A fraction of a second later, cosmic inflation stretched space far faster than light, seeding a nearly flat cosmos and birthing the density fluctuations that grew into galaxies.



Formation of Atoms to Galaxies

In the first minutes, nuclear reactions forged hydrogen, helium, and traces of lithium—primordial nucleosynthesis. After a cosmic "dark age," the first stars ignited, reionizing the universe by a billion years in. Galaxies gravitated into clusters and filaments, forming the cosmic web.



Composition: Visible & Invisible Matter

Ordinary atoms make up only ~5% of the universe. Dark matter—which interacts via gravity but not light—accounts for ~27%, while dark energy, discovered in 1998, makes up ~68% and drives accelerated expansion.



Expansion & Geometry

Since the Big Bang, space has been expanding; galaxies recede faster the farther away they are—Hubble’s law. This expansion is accelerating due to dark energy. The universe appears flat and infinite on cosmological scales.



Galaxies, Stars & Nebulae

Galaxies come in spirals, ellipticals, and irregulars. For example, the Whirlpool Galaxy (M51) shows classic spiral structure. Nebulae—like the Eagle, Orion, and Tarantula—are stellar nurseries where gas ionized by hot stars glows and triggers new star birth.



Cosmic Events & Phenomena

The universe hosts black holes, neutron star collisions, gamma-ray bursts, fast radio bursts, and shock waves from supernovae. These violent events seed heavy elements—like gold and platinum—in stellar collisions.



Probing the Cosmos

Instruments like WMAP, Planck, Hubble, JWST, and SPHEREx study cosmic microwave background, galaxies, and early star formation. SPHEREx will map 450 million galaxies to test inflation and locate water in space.



What Lies Ahead?

Wonders yet to explore include dark matter, dark energy, first stars, black holes, exoplanets, multiverse theories, and the ultimate fate of cosmic expansion—whether the universe keeps expanding, freezes, ripples, or recollapses.



Quick Facts

• Age: ≈13.8 billion years
• Observable radius: ≈46 billion light-years
• Galaxies: 100 billion–2 trillion
• Matter: 5% normal, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy
• Stars: >10²³ (~100 billion trillion)
• Expansion accelerates thanks to dark energy
