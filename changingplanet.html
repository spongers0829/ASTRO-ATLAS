Satellite Observations & Climate Science

NASA operates 20+ satellites that collect data on temperature, greenhouse gases, ice, sea level, oceans, atmosphere, and land cover. This long-term record reveals unequivocal warming trends—2021 ranked among the warmest years globally (~1.1°C above 19th-century levels).



Evidence of Change

NASA's climate data, ice core records, and satellite imaging confirm an era of rapid change: Arctic ice has shrunk by ~50%, glaciers retreat globally, sea levels rise, and more frequent extreme weather—droughts, floods, wildfires—pose increasing hazards.



Time-Lapse Imaging & Visualizations

The “Images of Change” and Google Earth Timelapse showcase glaciers (like Iceland’s Okjökull), coastlines, shrinking lakes, urban expansion, and Arctic ice decline. These sequences help us see transformations over decades.



Interactive Tools & Resources

NASA’s "Climate Interactives", "Earth’s Vital Signs", "Eyes on the Earth", and "Global Ice Viewer" offer interactive exploration—visualizing ice loss, sea level rise, ocean warming, CO₂ levels, wildfires, and more.



Applications & Action

NASA and partners (e.g., NOAA, FEMA) use data for climate assessments and policy guidance. New missions in development (Greenhouse gas monitors, ocean current trackers, ice-sheet surveyors) aim to fill critical data gaps by 2030–2032.



Impact on Humanity

Rising temperatures intensify wildfires and coastal flooding; polar melting alters weather extremes. NASA tools empower communities—farmers, planners, conservationists—to adapt. Robotic probes also assess hidden ice-shelf melting in Antarctica.



Looking Ahead

NASA continues bolstering its Earth-observing fleet, advancing satellite technology, and integrating climate science into decision-making. Through the Earth Info Center and global outreach, it empowers public action and stewardship.
