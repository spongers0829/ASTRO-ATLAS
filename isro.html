ISRO

Origins & Mandate

ISRO evolved from INCOSPAR, set up by Dr. Vikram Sarabhai in 1962, and officially established in 1969. Under the Department of Space, ISRO's goal is to harness space technology—launch vehicles, satellites, and related applications—for communication, navigation, Earth monitoring, science, and national development.



Facilities & Centres

ISRO operates across multiple centres: VSSC (launch vehicles, Thiruvananthapuram), URSC (satellite design, Bengaluru), SDSC Sriharikota (launch site), LPSC (liquid propulsion), SAC (Ahmedabad), NRSC (Hyderabad), and more. Its commercial arm Antrix (and NSIL) manage international launches.



Launch Vehicle Prowess

ISRO has developed PSLV (polar launches), GSLV (geosynchronous launches), and LVM3 heavy lift. The upcoming Next Generation Launch Vehicle (NGLV), targeting modular, reusable capabilities by 2034–35, will support India's future space-station and deep-space aspirations.



Flagship Missions

Milestones include Aryabhata (1975), Rohini (1980), INSAT & IRS satellite constellations, Chandrayaan-1 (lunar water discovery), Mangalyaan/Mars Orbiter (2014), Chandrayaan-3 (2023 lunar soft landing), and Aditya-L1 (first solar mission).



Human Spaceflight: Gaganyaan

The Gaganyaan program aims to send Indian astronauts into space. The first uncrewed test flew in 2023, and crewed missions—including humanoid Vyommitra—are scheduled beginning late 2025. A national space station (Bharatiya Antariksh Station) is planned by 2028.



Recent Breakthroughs

In January 2025, ISRO demonstrated satellite docking with SpaDeX, becoming the fourth nation to do so. Chairman V. Narayanan announced 10 military and surveillance satellites support national security, with India launching 131 satellites so far.



Social & Commercial Impact

ISRO’s EDUSAT, C-band communication, telemedicine, and remote sensing technologies support disaster response, agricultural monitoring, and rural connectivity. Its PSLV has deployed over 300 foreign satellites, earning global reputation. ISRO fosters private space startups like Skyroot and Agnikul via IN-SPACe.



Future Vision

ISRO plans include Chandrayaan-4 (sample return), Chandrayaan-5 (with JAXA), Gaganyaan crewed missions (2026+), Aditya-L1 solar operations, Venus and Mars sample return, and India’s own space station. The NGLV will power deeper exploration, aligning with a global space market projected to surge to $44 bn.



Quick Facts
