Astronaut Training

Astronaut training encompasses comprehensive preparation—physical, technical, and psychological—to ready crew for missions aboard the ISS, Artemis lunar missions, and future deep-space journeys. Training includes simulations, underwater exercises, robotics, geology, and teamwork to ensure mission success and crew safety.

Core Training Areas

Physical Conditioning & Survival

Astronauts maintain rigorous fitness routines to ensure cardiovascular, muscular, and bone health in microgravity. Training includes water survival, wilderness skills, and aircraft water egress practice. These build resilience for launch, landing, and emergencies.



Neutral Buoyancy Lab (NBL)

The largest underwater astronaut training facility simulates the microgravity of space. Crews wear full spacesuits and perform simulated spacewalks—learning tool use, hardware installation, and emergency procedures—to prepare for the ISS and Artemis lunar EVAs.



Robotics & Equipment Handling

Handling robotic arms, docking systems, rovers, and sample-collection tools is central to training. Crews practice operating the Canadarm2, lunar rovers, and Mars simulation vehicles, mastering coordination, precision, and troubleshooting.



Simulations & Mission Operations

High-fidelity simulators recreate spacecraft controls—Shuttle, Orion, Dragon—and mission scenarios. Crews train for docking, aborts, landings, communications, and complex mission sequences in simulated mission control environments worldwide.



Geology & Planetary Science

Field training in volcanic terrains, deserts, and polar regions prepares astronauts to collect scientific samples and interpret planetary geology during Mars and Moon missions. Teams learn geology techniques and traversal planning under expert guidance.



Teamwork & Leadership

Psychological preparation and crew dynamics are vital. Multi-week isolation missions, underwater expeditions, and analog habitats (HI-SEAS, NEEMO) build leadership, communication, and resilience within diverse teams living and working under stress.



Medical & Emergency Skills

Zero-G first aid, CPR, and advanced life-support training prepare astronauts for in-flight and surface emergencies. Crews learn to treat injuries, diagnose issues, monitor health, and use medical devices in microgravity and habitat simulations.



Quick Facts

• Average training time before flight: 2–4 years
• NBL: 40 × 28 m, 12 m depth, replicates weightlessness
• EVAs practiced: Dozens per mission at NBL
• Analog missions: HI‑SEAS (Mars), NEEMO (underwater), CAVES (caves), 0G parabolic flights.
• Psychological evaluations begin decades in advance; teams train for Lunar Gateway and Artemis missions with lunar-focused simulations.
