Humans in Space

For over six decades, humans have journeyed beyond Earth—living, working, and exploring in orbit and afar. From Yuri Gagarin’s first flight in 1961 to the continuous presence aboard the ISS since 2000, human spaceflight combines daring, science, and international aspiration. Today’s crews push farther, stay longer, and prepare the way for Artemis, lunar returns, and missions to Mars.

Milestones & Records

Human spaceflight began in April 1961 with Yuri Gagarin in orbit. Sally Ride became the first American woman in space in 1983, and Guion Bluford the first African American later that year . Christina Koch holds the record for longest female mission (328 days) , while Russia’s Valery Polyakov spent 437 days on Mir . Cumulatively, Oleg Kononenko leads with 1,111 days in space.



Life Aboard the ISS

Since November 2, 2000, more than 241 crew members from 19 countries have lived aboard the ISS, conducting over 3,000 experiments . Modules include crew quarters, labs, a gym, and the Cupola for Earth observation :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}. Microgravity requires creative adaptations—from liquid spices to vacuum toilets. Astronauts exercise 2 hours daily to prevent muscle and bone loss .



Health & Human Body

Spaceflight affects bone density, muscle mass, vision, circulation, sleep, and immunity. NASA monitors these changes and tests countermeasures—exercise, nutrition, LED lighting, and medicine . Studies like the Twins Study (Scott & Mark Kelly) show most health changes reverse after return, but radiation and microbiome effects require ongoing research .



Operations & Missions

Missions last from days to over a year. Recent Starliner missions extended planned eight-day flights to 286 days . Crew rotations are handled via SpaceX Crew Dragon and Boeing Starliner under NASA’s Commercial Crew Program . Expedition crews of 3–7 usually stay six months; larger crews returned after shuttle retirement



Psychology & Togetherness

Isolation and confinement pose mental-health challenges. NASA uses sleep tracking, journaling, VR relaxation, communal meals, and cultural talks to support morale :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}. Crews build camaraderie through shared meals, personal mementos, and rituals. Training missions like NEEMO and HI-SEAS simulate deep-space isolation .



Future & Artemis

Artemis missions are training a new "Turtles" class of astronauts for lunar and Mars missions . Future plans include rotating crews to the Lunar Gateway, extended moon stays, and missions farther into deep space—all building on decades of human spaceflight experience.



Quick Facts

• Total humans in space: 721 (102 women)
• ISS continuous crew since Nov 2000: 20 years
• Longest single mission: 437 days (Polyakov)
• ISS crew size: 7 members
• Daily exercise: 2 hours; crew height often increases 2–5 cm
• Countermeasures: LED lighting, resistive exercise, radiation protection, medical kits
